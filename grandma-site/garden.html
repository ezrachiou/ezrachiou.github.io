My grandma has a garden of her own in the city.

Around her apartment, plants live in the spots with the best sun.

Sit with the plants

✳

You can tell when plants are loved.

It’s a quiet, consistent care. Hours, days, years spent tending to something which will not speak yet returns your love all the same.

When I was maybe sixteen, my grandma and I stood together in the balcony. It’s a style of apartment architecture I haven’t run into yet in America: a decently spacious rectangle of glass panes and windows, the upper–middle portion of which slide open and closed horizontally. Sliding doors in the living room which lead out to its cool tile floors—the closest comparison may be a sunroom–greenhouse hybrid, though I haven’t really been in either. This balcony was where we hung laundry to dry in the warmth of summer, where my grandma showed me to pull washed clothes taut so that they’d be wrinkle–free when we took them down to fold. This was also where we were when she pointed to each of her sun–warmed plants, told me how she’d cared for them. Likely what they were, too, but I didn’t know the right words. I can’t remember as many details as I want—It’s been 10 years or so, of course, but I was trying to keep up as well. To piece together meaning with what Mandarin I knew, which is a process in itself. A charged experience, too, when you have the shame of not knowing, of failing to connect because of your own inadequacy. Well. I still remember when she showed me to her African milk tree plant (I learned later what it was called) and told me she’d had it for eight years now. I’d been amazed, likely exclaiming 哇！to show just that, and it was easy to tell she'd loved it a lot. It’d grown so tall—nearly as tall as me, including the pot. Raising plants to be so happy is a labor of love, of careful observation and consistency. She saw things in me quickly, too, and cared for me in a way my other family hadn’t known how (by no fault of their own). This was the same summer she took me to the doctor because I’d been sleeping til 12—I’d spend nights on that same balcony, surrounded by her plants, watching the sky until sometimes 2 or 3 AM. I struggled, still, but it was around then I decided to live because I didn’t want to make her sad. I could kill myself after she was gone, so that she wouldn’t have to bear the weight.

There was a special kind of peace on that balcony. At the time I’d thought it was just the sky, the city lights, the quiet of the nighttime with few neighbors coming and going. The life in every window, a gentle red fading in and out atop each building to warn planes, the clouds that passed to reveal faint stars in the light–polluted sky. I know now, too, that it was my grandma’s plants. Plants have a way of soothing your heart even without you realizing, just by their presence alone. I think it was the same with my grandma and I. All I’d known, even when I didn’t have the words for what was happening inside me, is that I wanted to be with her longer. I'm older now and know much more, can see so many little ways she'd tried to lift my spirits when things were bad. I often think to myself, resent even, that I didn't know how loved I was. Looking back though, I believe some part of me felt it all the same. We're both still here because of her.
